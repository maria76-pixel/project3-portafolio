The problem

Inventory losses with no financial visibility

The client operated a product distribution business across four Latin American countries — Mexico, Colombia, Brazil, and Peru. Units were shipped from a central warehouse, handed to local partners, and deployed to end users. The problem: by the time financial reports were produced at month-end, inventory discrepancies had already become losses. There was no system connecting what was happening physically to what was showing up financially.

The client needed more than a dashboard. They needed a control system — something that could detect a missing shipment in Mexico, explain why a margin dropped in Brazil, and model what expanding to a new country would actually cost.

System model

Connecting inventory to finance at every stage

I designed the system around a single core principle: every unit that moves must have a corresponding financial entry, and any divergence between the two is an alert. The inventory flow — Supplier → Warehouse → Shipped → Country Partner → Active → Returned — is mirrored by a financial flow at each stage, so a missing unit isn't just a logistics problem, it immediately shows up as a cost and margin impact.

  • 10,240 total units tracked across 4 countries simultaneously
  • Each shipment stage cross-referenced against partner receipts within 24 hours
  • Any variance above 1% triggers an automatic alert with financial impact estimate
  • Country-level P&L updated weekly, not monthly — issues caught in days, not weeks
